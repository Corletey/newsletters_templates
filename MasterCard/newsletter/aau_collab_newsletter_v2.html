◆  01   About the Convening

A strategic platform to accelerate African-led climate action

The Africa Climate Collaborative (ACC) convened its 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya, from 20–24 April 2026 under the theme “From Ambition to Action: Scaling African Youth-Led Climate Solutions.” Bringing together representatives from the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the Association of African Universities (AAU), the Mastercard Foundation, youth leaders, researchers, innovators and ecosystem partners, the convening served as a strategic platform to accelerate the Collaborative’s vision for climate resilience and sustainable development across Africa.

The convening placed particular emphasis on the role of young people as drivers of climate action and green economic transformation. Discussions focused on climate innovation and entrepreneurship, research and knowledge generation, workforce development, policy engagement, and strengthening partnerships capable of scaling African-led climate solutions.

This special newsletter edition captures key highlights, insights and lessons from the convening — showcasing how universities, development partners, innovators and young leaders are working together to translate ambition into action and build a more resilient and sustainable future for Africa.

◆  02   Youth Leadership

Young Africans shaping climate solutions

Young people are increasingly at the forefront of climate action across Africa — developing innovative solutions, driving community initiatives and advocating for a more sustainable future. At the convening, youth leaders, innovators and programme representatives came together to reflect on the challenges facing their communities and explore practical pathways for scaling youth-led climate action.

The dedicated youth session reinforced a central message that echoed throughout the week: young people are not merely beneficiaries of climate programmes — they are active partners and leaders in shaping Africa’s climate future. Delegates shared initiatives already making a difference, including climate-smart agriculture and agroforestry programmes, digital platforms connecting farmers to markets, maritime cooperatives promoting sustainable livelihoods, and leadership programmes strengthening emerging innovators and entrepreneurs.

While celebrating these successes, participants also reflected on the barriers that continue to limit youth participation and impact. Limited access to funding, mentorship, technology and policy support remain significant challenges. Delegates noted the need for clearer pathways for engagement and greater opportunities for young people to contribute meaningfully to decision-making at local, national and continental levels.

A recurring theme was the importance of moving from ambition to action. Many promising youth-led solutions already exist, but greater investment is needed to help these initiatives grow. Strengthening mentorship networks, expanding access to financing and creating more opportunities for collaboration were identified as essential steps. Institutions such as the AAU and partner universities were recognised for their potential to strengthen research-to-practice linkages and create environments where young people can develop and scale their ideas.

Participants called for structured mechanisms that allow young people to contribute consistently to programme design, implementation and governance — ensuring youth perspectives remain embedded within climate initiatives. The Nairobi convening reaffirmed that with the right support, resources and opportunities, young people have the capacity to drive transformative change, and that empowering them is an investment not only in the future, but in the present.

“Africa does not lack talent, ideas or ambition. The continent’s climate future will depend on its ability to build stronger systems that connect people, institutions, knowledge and investments.”

— Opening Session, ACC 2026 Convening

◆  03   Climate Leadership

Rethinking climate leadership in Africa

The convening opened with a compelling call for a new approach to climate action — one that moves beyond isolated interventions towards coordinated, systems-level solutions. The opening session explored how Africa can strengthen climate resilience by connecting research, innovation, policy and community action, agreeing that climate change is not simply an environmental issue but a complex challenge affecting livelihoods, education, public health, food security and economic development.

Moderated by Prof. Frederick Ato Armah, Director of Research and Programmes at the AAU, the discussion emphasised the need for collaboration across institutions and sectors. Participants noted that while Africa contributes only a small share of global greenhouse gas emissions, it continues to experience some of the most severe climate-related impacts — including food insecurity, displacement and economic vulnerability — requiring integrated solutions that cut across disciplines, sectors and institutions.

The discussion highlighted the continent’s greatest asset — its youthful population — while stressing the need to expand access to financing, mentorship, leadership opportunities and decision-making spaces. Universities were identified as critical actors: beyond teaching and research, higher education institutions were encouraged to serve as ecosystem builders connecting researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, entrepreneurs and communities.

Participants reflected on persistent challenges: many promising ideas struggle to move beyond pilot stages due to limited access to patient, flexible capital, and translating research into policies, products and community solutions remains difficult. Stronger links between academia, governments, the private sector and development partners — and greater attention to women, rural communities and persons with disabilities — were identified as critical to accelerating an inclusive, resilient transition.

◆  04   Green Economy

Preparing youth for Africa’s green economy

Green Jobs Project   University of Cambridge  ·  Two-year research initiative

As Africa seeks to build climate-resilient economies, a key question is emerging: are young people equipped with the skills to thrive in the green economy? This question was at the heart of a session showcasing findings from the Green Jobs Project, a two-year research initiative led by the University of Cambridge with partner universities and youth organisations. A distinguishing feature was its youth-centred approach — young people were active partners in shaping research questions, collecting data, analysing findings and sharing results.

The research highlighted vast potential across renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, waste management, ecosystem restoration and environmental services — but also revealed significant barriers. Low awareness of green jobs, limited access to finance for young entrepreneurs, social perceptions that view sectors such as agriculture and waste management as less desirable, gender inequalities and persistent skills gaps all continue to restrict opportunities.

To address these challenges, the project called for greater investment in skills development, entrepreneurship support and climate-focused financing, alongside education and training systems better aligned with labour-market demands. A panel discussion that followed asked whether universities are preparing students for today’s green jobs or for the future green economy Africa is striving to build.

A key message: universities must move beyond traditional academic models and equip students with practical, entrepreneurial and interdisciplinary skills, forging stronger partnerships with industry, governments and communities, integrating sustainability across teaching and research, and recognising indigenous knowledge systems as valuable contributors to climate solutions.

◆  05   Innovation

Turning ideas into action

How can African universities move beyond generating knowledge to creating solutions that address real-world climate challenges? Held under the theme “Climate Innovation in Action,” a high-level panel explored how higher education institutions can strengthen climate innovation, support entrepreneurship and prepare young people to lead Africa’s green transition. A key message: universities must strengthen connections between research, industry, communities and policymakers, since many valuable innovations struggle to move beyond academic settings into practical application.

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the AAU, noted that many institutions are increasingly investing in climate-related research, innovation hubs, entrepreneurship programmes and skills development — but emphasised the importance of enabling environments that allow innovations to be developed, tested and scaled. Sharing her experience as a young entrepreneur, Laura Kasuni Wathe observed that many graduates struggle to transition from education to enterprise, calling for greater emphasis on mentorship, systems thinking, leadership development and community engagement.

Participants explored the concept of “living laboratories,” where universities work directly with communities and entrepreneurs to develop and test solutions in real-world settings, and challenged one another to rethink what innovation means — highlighting the value of indigenous knowledge and locally developed practices. Inclusion was central: ensuring women, persons with disabilities, displaced populations and young people from underserved communities are meaningfully represented within climate innovation ecosystems.

The session reinforced a key message of the convening: addressing climate challenges requires more than research alone. It demands collaboration, inclusion, and a commitment to transforming ideas into practical solutions that create lasting impact for communities across Africa.

“Africa does not lack ideas. What we lack are systems that help those ideas grow.”

— Delta40 Site Visit, Nairobi

◆  06   Field Visit

From ideas to impact: lessons from Delta40

The Delta40 site visit gave participants a firsthand look at the realities of climate entrepreneurship and the challenges innovators face in transforming promising ideas into scalable solutions. Discussions with founders and ecosystem builders highlighted a common challenge: innovation is not the problem — execution is.

A key focus was the so-called “valley of death,” the stage where many ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure and insufficient operational support — particularly acute in clean energy, climate technology and sustainable food systems. Many founders described themselves as “over-mentored and under-capitalised”: while training programmes and pitch competitions are abundant, access to patient capital, technical expertise and strategic business support remains limited.

The Delta40 model offers a different approach. Rather than short-term acceleration, the venture studio works closely with entrepreneurs over extended periods, offering embedded support in finance, operations and commercialisation. Discussions also highlighted the gap between research and commercialisation within African universities — many researchers develop solutions with significant potential but lack the structures and partnerships needed to bring them to market.

Existing collaborations between Delta40 and institutions such as the University of Cambridge demonstrate the value of linking research, innovation and enterprise development. The visit reinforced a central message: Africa has no shortage of talent, ambition or ideas — what is needed are stronger systems that support execution, provide long-term backing for entrepreneurs, and connect research with real-world application.

◆  07   Communications

Strengthening universities’ collective voice

Effective climate action depends not only on innovative solutions but on the ability to communicate them to the people who need them most. Communications leads and programme representatives from partner institutions convened to strengthen alignment on strategic communications, branding and impact storytelling — with representatives from the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the University of Cape Town, the University of Cambridge, the AAU and the Mastercard Foundation.

A central theme was the need for a more coordinated approach. Participants acknowledged that valuable research, innovation and community impact often remain underrepresented due to fragmented communication efforts. By adopting a shared storytelling framework, the institutions aim to increase the reach and influence of their collective work. The University of Ghana highlighted plans for a cohesive brand identity and youth-focused climate stories; Makerere University emphasised inclusion and accessibility; the University of Cambridge outlined a central platform for grants, opportunities and impact stories; and the University of Cape Town underscored climate education and interdisciplinary research through its Graduate School of Climate and Climate Action Innovation Lab.

The session reinforced that communication is not merely a supporting function but a strategic tool for advancing climate action. Through collaboration, strategic storytelling and inclusive engagement, the partner institutions are working to amplify a collective voice for climate resilience across the continent.

◆  08   Landmark Workshop

A landmark communications & branding workshop

The ACC took an important step towards strengthening its collective identity with its first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop in Nairobi. Bringing together communications professionals, programme coordinators and institutional representatives from partner universities, the workshop provided a platform to align communication priorities, strengthen storytelling and enhance collaboration across the network.

Opening the session, ACC Lead Thuli Montana underscored the importance of communication in advancing the Collaborative’s mission — established to support higher education institutions in driving climate-positive growth through research, innovation, skills development and youth empowerment. As the initiative enters a new phase of implementation, she emphasised the need to intentionally capture and share stories that demonstrate impact and inspire action.

A key theme was the importance of speaking with one collective voice. Sessions focused on storytelling frameworks, audience engagement, communication planning, monitoring and evaluation, and the practical application of branding guidelines. Particular attention was given to establishing a consistent visual identity — reviewing branding protocols across digital content, publications, events, promotional materials and institutional communications.

The workshop concluded with a renewed commitment to strengthen coordination, share knowledge and amplify impact — leaving participants with a clearer roadmap for implementing the ACC’s communication strategy and ensuring greater consistency in branding, storytelling and audience engagement.

◆  09   Key Takeaways

Five messages from Nairobi

Across discussions, workshops and site engagements, several key messages emerged from the 2026 Convening.

01  Systems-level solutions

Addressing climate challenges demands stronger connections between research, policy, innovation, financing and community action.

02  Youth at the centre

Expanding access to skills development, entrepreneurship support, mentorship and financing is essential for unlocking youth potential.

03  Universities’ critical role

Higher education institutions are uniquely positioned to generate knowledge, nurture innovation and prepare young people for the green economy.

04  Innovation ecosystems

Effective solutions require long-term support systems that enable ideas to move from research and pilot stages to sustainable impact.

05  Strategic communication is vital

Amplifying African-led climate stories and showcasing impact can strengthen partnerships, influence policy and inspire action. The ACC’s first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop reinforced the importance of a coordinated collective voice.

◆  10   What comes next

Moving from ambition to action

As the Africa Climate Collaborative enters the next phase of implementation, partner institutions will continue advancing research, innovation, climate entrepreneurship, skills development and youth-focused programmes across the continent. Upcoming activities will include the rollout of climate-related training programmes, support for green enterprise development, continued research and fellowship activities, expansion of innovation partnerships, and enhanced engagement with policymakers and communities.

Building on the momentum generated in Nairobi, the Collaborative remains committed to translating ambition into action and strengthening pathways for young Africans to contribute meaningfully to climate resilience and sustainable development.
