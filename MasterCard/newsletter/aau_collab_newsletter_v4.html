2026 Nairobi Convening Newsletter • June 2026

Lead Story • Nairobi, Kenya • 20–24 April 2026

A Defining Moment for African Climate Action

Africa is home to the world's youngest population — and some of its most severe climate impacts. Across the continent, young people are not waiting for solutions. They are building them. The Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening, held in Nairobi from 20–24 April, was designed to change the equation: moving from ambition to action.

The Africa Climate Collaborative is one of the Mastercard Foundation's seven Collaboratives driving systems change in critical sectors for Africa's socio-economic development. It brings together four leading universities — the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, and Makerere University — along with the Association of African Universities (AAU) as its backbone organisation.

Bringing together researchers, youth leaders, entrepreneurs, and decision-makers from partner institutions, the convening asked a harder question: What does it actually take to move from ambition to action?

Over five days, participants moved beyond conversation to tackle the systems, partnerships, and investments needed to turn African-led climate solutions into lasting impact.
 
 

Youth Session

What We Heard: Young Africans Are Not Waiting

Young people are not merely participants in climate programmes — they are driving them. A dedicated youth session brought together innovators, entrepreneurs, and programme representatives from across the continent to share what is already working.

“Many of us are over-mentored and under-capitalised.”

— Youth Participant, ACC Convening 2026

In their communities, young people are leading climate-smart agriculture, building digital platforms that connect farmers to markets, establishing maritime cooperatives, and designing leadership programmes for emerging climate innovators. These are not pilot projects — they are proofs of concept.

What emerged was a clear demand: young people want to move beyond being consulted. They want to co-design, govern, and lead — with structured mechanisms that allow them to contribute consistently to programme design, implementation, and decision-making.
 

Opening Session

Rethinking Climate Leadership in Africa

The convening opened with a compelling call for a new approach to climate action — one that moves beyond isolated interventions towards coordinated, systems-level solutions. Moderated by Prof. Frederick Ato Armah, Director of Research and Programmes at the AAU, the session explored how Africa can strengthen climate resilience by connecting research, innovation, policy and community action.

Participants agreed that climate change is a complex challenge affecting livelihoods, education, public health and food security. While Africa contributes only a small share of global emissions, it experiences some of the most severe climate-related impacts — requiring integrated solutions across disciplines, sectors and institutions. Universities were identified as critical ecosystem builders connecting researchers, policymakers, industry leaders and communities.
 
 

Site Visit • Nairobi

Africa Does Not Lack Talent, Ideas, or Ambition

Delta40 Venture Studio, Nairobi — What Needs Strengthening Are the Systems

During a site visit to Delta40, a venture studio based in Nairobi, participants saw firsthand what it takes to build climate ventures in Africa. Discussions with founders and ecosystem builders revealed a sobering reality: innovation is not the problem. Execution and scale are.

The visit focused on the "valley of death" — the stage where promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure, and insufficient operational support. Delta40 offers a different model: rather than short-term acceleration, the studio works closely with entrepreneurs over extended periods, offering embedded support in finance, operations, and commercialisation.

“I didn't set out to build an organisation. I set out to solve a problem. But the systems weren't there. So I had to build them myself.”

— Founder, Delta40 Entrepreneur

The site visit reinforced a central message of the convening: Africa has no shortage of talent, ambition, or innovative ideas. What is needed are stronger systems that support execution, provide long-term backing for entrepreneurs, and connect research with real-world application.
 
 

Research Session

Are Universities Preparing Students for Today’s Green Jobs?

A session on the Green Jobs Project showcased a two-year research initiative led by the University of Cambridge in collaboration with partner universities and youth organisations across Africa.

The research was distinguished by its approach: young people were not simply research participants but active partners in shaping questions, collecting data, analysing findings, and sharing results.

A key message emerged: universities must move beyond traditional academic models and equip students with practical, entrepreneurial, and interdisciplinary skills.

“If we keep teaching students the way we were taught, we will prepare them for a world that no longer exists.”
 

Innovation Panel

Research Alone Is Not Enough. Universities Must Become Ecosystem Builders.

A high-level panel brought together representatives from academia, industry, development organisations, and youth entrepreneurship to explore how higher education institutions can strengthen climate innovation and support Africa's green transition.

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the Association of African Universities, stressed that partnerships are essential: “Universities cannot do this alone. We need industry, we need government, we need communities, and we need young people at the table.”

The discussion also explored “living laboratories” — universities working directly with communities and entrepreneurs to develop and test solutions in real-world settings — and underscored the importance of ensuring that women, persons with disabilities, and young people from underserved communities are meaningfully represented within climate innovation ecosystems.
 
 

Communications Workshop

A Collective Voice: Strengthening Communications Across the Collaborative

Communications leads and programme representatives from all partner institutions convened the Collaborative's first in-person Communications and Branding engagement. The session brought together representatives from the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, Makerere University, AAU, and the Mastercard Foundation.

“The Africa Climate Collaborative was established to support higher education institutions in driving climate-positive growth. But if we don’t tell the story — if we don’t show what is working, who is leading, what we are learning — the impact remains invisible.”

— Thuli Montana, Lead, Africa Climate Collaborative

The workshop reinforced a critical understanding: communication is not a supporting function. It is a strategic tool for advancing climate action. Participants left with a shared storytelling framework and a clearer roadmap for greater consistency in branding, storytelling, and audience engagement.

Institution Communication Priorities

University of Ghana: Cohesive brand identity and youth-focused climate stories; promoting green enterprise success

Makerere University: Inclusion and accessibility for underserved youth; strengthening impact measurement

University of Cape Town: Climate education and innovation lab impact stories through the Graduate School of Climate

Key Takeaways

What We Learned
1

Climate change is a development and systems challenge. Its impacts span employment, food security, education, fiscal stability, and social cohesion. Addressing it requires structural responses that integrate economic transformation, social inclusion, and institutional reform.
2

Youth agency consistently strengthens outcomes. Where young people are positioned as co-creators, implementers, and knowledge producers — rather than passive beneficiaries — solutions are more relevant, durable, and scalable.
3

Africa does not lack ideas — it lacks systems. The continent has no shortage of talent, ambition, or innovative solutions. What is needed are coordinated, adaptive systems that can translate these assets into durable, equitable impact at scale.
4

Finance remains the primary constraint. Many promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure, and insufficient operational support. The challenge is not the absolute volume of funding but the availability of appropriate financial instruments — particularly patient capital.
5

Equity and inclusion must be structural, not symbolic. Inclusion was framed not merely as access but as power and value capture — who controls finance, research priorities, enterprise ownership, and decision-making. Those most affected by climate change must be empowered to shape agendas and lead.
6

If you are findable, you are fundable. Governance coherence, evidence-based learning, and strategic communications are mutually reinforcing systems. The Collaborative's first in-person Communications Workshop underscored that visibility is a precondition for impact.
 
 

Looking Ahead

What Comes Next

The conversations in Nairobi were not an endpoint. They were a beginning — the moment when the Collaborative shifted from alignment to activation. Commitments made in that room are already being followed up.

Venture studios like Delta40 have committed to deepening their engagement with university-based innovators. Industry partners have pledged to strengthen pathways between graduates and green employment opportunities.

Upcoming activities will include the rollout of climate-related training programmes, support for green enterprise development, continued research and fellowship activities, expansion of innovation partnerships, and enhanced engagement with policymakers and communities.
