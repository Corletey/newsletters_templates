About the Convening

Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening — Nairobi

The Africa Climate Collaborative (ACC) convened its 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya, from 20–24 April 2026 under the theme "From Ambition to Action: Scaling African Youth-Led Climate Solutions."

Bringing together representatives from the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the AAU, the Mastercard Foundation, youth leaders, researchers, and innovators — the convening served as a strategic platform to accelerate the Collaborative's vision for climate resilience across Africa.

Youth Leadership

Young Africans Shaping Climate Solutions

At the dedicated youth session, delegates reinforced a central message: young people are not merely beneficiaries of climate programmes — they are active partners and leaders.

Initiatives presented included climate-smart agriculture, digital farmer platforms, maritime cooperatives, and leadership programmes designed to strengthen the capacity of emerging climate innovators.

Participants called for structured mechanisms that allow young people to contribute consistently to programme design and governance.
Climate Leadership

Rethinking Climate Leadership in Africa

Moderated by Prof. Frederick Ato Armah, Director of Research & Programmes at AAU, the opening session called for a systems-level approach — connecting research, innovation, policy, and community action.

Universities were identified as critical ecosystem builders: connecting researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, and communities to accelerate the translation of research into practical, scalable climate solutions across Africa.

“

Africa does not lack talent, ideas, or ambition. The continent's climate future will depend on its ability to build stronger systems that connect people, institutions, knowledge, and investments.

— Opening Session, ACC 2026 Convening, Nairobi
 
Green Jobs Project

Preparing Youth for Africa's Green Economy

University of Cambridge  ·  Two-Year Research Initiative

A youth-centred research initiative where young people were not merely participants but active partners in shaping research questions and analysing findings. The project identified vast opportunities in renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, waste management, and ecosystem restoration.

Key barriers surfaced: low awareness of green career pathways, limited access to finance, and social perceptions limiting uptake in sectors like agriculture. Universities must equip students with entrepreneurial, interdisciplinary, and practical skills aligned to market demands.
Climate Innovation

Universities and Climate Innovation in Africa

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of AAU, stressed that while institutions invest in climate innovation hubs and entrepreneurship programmes, stronger partnerships are needed to develop, test, and scale innovations for impact.

The concept of "living laboratories" — where universities work directly with communities to develop solutions in real-world settings — was highlighted as a powerful model for grounding innovations in local realities. Inclusion of women, youth, and underserved populations was emphasised as essential.
 
Delta40 Site Visit

From Ideas to Impact: Lessons on Scaling Climate Innovation

The Delta40 visit surfaced a recurring frustration in Africa's entrepreneurial ecosystem: innovators are "over-mentored and under-capitalised." While training and pitch competitions are abundant, access to patient capital and strategic business support remains scarce — leaving many ventures stuck in the "valley of death."

Delta40's embedded, long-term venture studio model offers an alternative — providing hands-on support in finance, operations, and commercialisation. Participants agreed: Africa has no shortage of talent. What is needed are stronger systems that support execution.
 
Communications & Branding

Strengthening Universities' Collective Voice for Climate Resilience

Communications leads from all partner institutions gathered in Nairobi to align on strategic communications, branding, and impact storytelling. The central challenge: valuable research and innovation remain underrepresented due to fragmented communication efforts.

The ACC's first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop, opened by ACC Lead Thuli Montana, operationalised the Collaborative's communication strategy — covering storytelling frameworks, audience engagement, branding guidelines, and monitoring of impact.

Key Takeaways from the 2026 Convening

1

Young people must be positioned as partners and leaders, not just beneficiaries, in climate action programmes.
2

Universities must move beyond traditional academic models and serve as innovation ecosystems connecting research, policy, and practice.
3

Africa's green transition demands greater investment in green skills development, entrepreneurship support, and climate-focused financing.
4

Promising ideas need patient capital, long-term mentorship, and market access to grow beyond pilot stage.
5

Inclusive climate action must centre women, rural communities, and underrepresented groups in decision-making and innovation ecosystems.
6

A coordinated, unified communications strategy is essential to amplifying African-led climate solutions and shaping policy discourse.

Moving from Ambition to Action — What Comes Next

The 2026 Convening has laid a foundation for accelerated action. Partner institutions will work to implement commitments made in Nairobi — expanding youth leadership pathways, strengthening university-industry partnerships, scaling climate innovation hubs, and amplifying African scholarship on the global stage.
