Africa is home to the world’s youngest population - and some of its most severe climate impacts. Across the continent, young people are not waiting for solutions. They are building them: climate-smart farms, renewable energy ventures, digital platforms connecting farmers to markets, and community-led adaptation initiatives. Yet too often, these innovations remain underfunded, disconnected from institutions, and unable to scale.

The Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening, held in Nairobi, Kenya from 20 - 24 April, was designed to change that.

For context, the Africa Climate Collaborative is one of the Mastercard Foundation’s seven Collaboratives driving systems change in critical sectors for Africa’s socio-economic development. The Collaborative brings together four leading universities - the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, and Makerere University - along with key institutional partners, including the Association of African Universities (AAU), which serves as the Collaborative’s backbone. Bringing together researchers, youth leaders, entrepreneurs, and decision-makers from partner institutions, the convening asked a harder question: What does it actually take to move from ambition to action? The 2026 Convening was designed to catalyze this transition, building from strategic co-creation to robust operationalization. The gathering served as the pivotal platform to ratify the core frameworks, accelerate joint initiatives, and solidify the internal architecture required for sustained impact. The convening was a strategic working session dedicated to aligning efforts, finalizing foundational tools, and strengthening the partnerships at the heart of the Collaborative’s mission. Serving to showcase its initial impact across the university partners, it also provided a dynamic platform for peer learning, knowledge sharing, and strategic engagement with key stakeholders, including the youth leading Africa’s climate-resilient future.

Over five days, participants moved beyond conversation to tackle the systems, partnerships, and investments needed to turn African-led climate solutions into lasting impact.
 
Youth Leadership

What We Heard: Young Africans Are Not Waiting

Young people are not merely participants in climate programmes - they are driving them. This message resonated throughout the convening, particularly during a dedicated youth session that brought together innovators, entrepreneurs, and programme representatives from across the continent.

Participants shared what is already working. In their communities, young people are leading climate-smart agriculture and agroforestry initiatives, building digital platforms that connect farmers to markets, establishing maritime cooperatives that promote sustainable livelihoods, and designing leadership programmes that strengthen the capacity of emerging climate innovators. These are not pilot projects. They are proofs of concept - demonstrations that when young people are trusted and supported, they deliver.

But the session also surfaced what is not working.

“Many of us are over-mentored and under-capitalised,” one participant observed - a sentiment that echoed throughout the convening. Training programmes and pitch competitions are abundant. What young innovators need is different: patient capital, long-term mentorship, access to technology, and pathways to influence policy decisions that affect their futures.

Participants named the barriers plainly. Limited access to funding remains the most persistent obstacle. Mentorship networks are often fragmented or inaccessible. Policy spaces remain closed to young people, even when policies directly affect their livelihoods. And too often, youth engagement is consultative rather than substantive - a single session rather than a seat at the table.

What emerged was a clear demand: young people want to move beyond being consulted. They want to co-design, govern, and lead. They want structured mechanisms that allow them to contribute consistently to programme design, implementation, and decision-making - not just at events, but across the life of every initiative that claims to serve them.

Africa does not lack talent, ideas, or ambition. The continent’s climate future will depend on its ability to build stronger systems that connect people, institutions, knowledge, and investments.

— Opening Session, Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening, Nairobi
Foundation Perspective

Foundation Perspective – Ashley Collier, Mastercard Foundation

Ashley Collier, Director for Higher Education for Transformation at the Mastercard Foundation, opened the Africa Climate Collaborative Convening in Nairobi with a high-level overview of the Foundation’s Africa Works strategy. She highlighted the Foundation’s commitment to supporting Africa’s youth and universities in driving climate resilience, innovation, and socio-economic transformation.

Collier emphasized the importance of coordinated action to scale climate solutions across the continent, noting that Africa’s youth are central to these efforts. She framed universities as critical hubs for knowledge, research, and talent development, capable of fostering climate-smart initiatives, entrepreneurship, and policy engagement.

The Foundation’s strategy, as outlined by Collier, seeks to integrate climate considerations across education, financial inclusion, and workforce development programs, ensuring young innovators have the resources, mentorship, and policy access to scale their solutions.

She encouraged participants to collaborate, innovate, and share lessons learned to accelerate systemic change and build long-term capacity for climate action across Africa.
Green Jobs Project

Are Universities Preparing Students for Today’s Green Jobs - or the Future Green Economy?

This question shaped a session on the Green Jobs Project, a two-year research initiative led by the University of Cambridge in collaboration with partner universities and youth organisations across Africa.

The research was distinguished by its approach: young people were not simply research participants but active partners in shaping questions, collecting data, analysing findings, and sharing results. This ensured that the experiences and perspectives of young people remained central throughout.

The findings confirmed both opportunity and challenge. Africa’s green economy holds vast potential across renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, waste management, ecosystem restoration, and environmental services. But significant barriers continue to limit youth participation.

During the panel discussion at the convening, it was emphasised that low awareness of green careers remains a persistent challenge. Many young people are unfamiliar with the opportunities emerging in green industries. Social perceptions also influence career choices - sectors like agriculture and waste management are often viewed as less desirable despite their growing importance. Gender inequalities, limited access to resources, and skills gaps further restrict opportunities for many young people.

The discussion called for greater investment in skills development, entrepreneurship support, and climate-focused financing mechanisms. Panellists emphasised the need for education and training systems better aligned with labour market demands, as well as policies that encourage innovation and green enterprise development.

A key message emerged: universities must move beyond traditional academic models and equip students with practical, entrepreneurial, and interdisciplinary skills. Stronger partnerships between universities, industry, governments, and communities are essential for ensuring that graduates are prepared to respond to evolving workforce needs. Indigenous knowledge systems, too, must be recognised as valuable contributors to environmental stewardship and climate solutions.

“If we keep teaching students the way we were taught, we will prepare them for a world that no longer exists.”
 
Climate Innovation

Research Alone Is Not Enough. Universities Must Become Ecosystem Builders.

A high-level panel discussion on climate innovation pushed this thinking further.

Bringing together representatives from academia, industry, development organisations, and youth entrepreneurship, the session explored how higher education institutions can strengthen climate innovation, support entrepreneurship, and prepare young people to lead Africa’s green transition.

The message was clear: universities cannot afford to remain ivory towers. They must become convenors - spaces where researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, entrepreneurs, and communities come together to co-create solutions.

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the Association of African Universities, noted that many institutions are increasingly investing in climate-related research, innovation hubs, and entrepreneurship programmes. But he stressed that partnerships are essential: “Universities cannot do this alone. We need industry, we need government, we need communities, and we need young people at the table.”

Laura Kasuni Wathe, a young entrepreneur, observed that many graduates face challenges when transitioning from education to enterprise development. She called for greater emphasis on mentorship, systems thinking, leadership development, and community engagement to help young people transform ideas into sustainable ventures. The discussion explored the concept of “living laboratories” - universities working directly with communities, entrepreneurs, and other stakeholders to develop and test solutions in real-world settings. Such approaches, participants agreed, ensure that innovations are grounded in local realities and respond to the needs of the communities they are intended to serve.

Inclusion was another central theme. Panelists underscored the importance of ensuring that women, persons with disabilities, displaced populations, and young people from underserved communities are meaningfully represented within climate innovation ecosystems. Building resilient and sustainable solutions requires the active participation of those most affected by climate-related challenges.

Ultimately, the session reinforced a key message of the convening: addressing climate challenges requires more than research alone. It demands collaboration, inclusion, and a commitment to transforming ideas into practical solutions that create lasting impact for communities across Africa.
 
Delta40 Site Visit

Africa Does Not Lack Talents, Ideas or Ambition. What Needs Strengthening Are Systems That Help Those Ideas and Talents Grow.

This message landed differently outside the conference room.

During a site visit to Delta40, a venture studio based in Nairobi, Kenya, participants saw firsthand what it takes to build climate ventures in Africa. Discussions with founders and ecosystem builders revealed a sobering reality: innovation is not the problem. Execution and scale are.

The visit focused on what entrepreneurs call the “valley of death” - the stage where promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure, and insufficient operational support. This challenge is particularly acute in sectors like clean energy, climate technology, and sustainable food systems, where entrepreneurs often require significant capital and long-term backing to scale their solutions.

Delta40 offers a different model. Rather than providing short-term acceleration, the venture studio works closely with entrepreneurs over extended periods, offering embedded support in finance, operations, and commercialisation. This hands-on approach helps ventures navigate the complex challenges of growth while building the capacity needed for long-term sustainability.

The visit also highlighted a persistent gap between research and commercialisation within African universities. Many researchers develop innovative solutions with significant potential - but lack the structures, partnerships, and support needed to bring those ideas to market.

“If strong institutions existed to support commercialisation, more researchers would be entrepreneurs and research findings won’t only stay in labs,” one participant reflected.

As one founder put it: “I didn’t set out to build an organisation. I set out to solve a problem. But the systems weren’t there. So, I had to build them myself.”

The site visit reinforced a central message of the convening: Africa has no shortage of talent, ambition, or innovative ideas. What is needed are stronger systems that support execution, provide long-term backing for entrepreneurs, and connect research with real-world application.
 
Communications & Branding

A Collective Voice: Strengthening Communications Across the Collaborative

Institution Priorities

U. of Ghana: Cohesive brand identity & youth-focused climate stories

Makerere: Inclusion & accessibility for underserved young people

Cambridge: Central platform for grants, opportunities & impact stories

Cape Town: Graduate School of Climate & Climate Action Innovation Lab

Each institution highlighted its priorities – including focus on strengthening project visibility through cohesive branding and sharing impact stories from researchers and fellows. Others included an emphasis on inclusion and accessibility, ensuring that climate opportunities reach underserved young people while strengthening systems for measuring and communicating impact.

Despite their different priorities, the institutions identified common challenges: translating complex research into accessible narratives, producing high-quality content consistently, measuring communication impact, and ensuring effective coordination across partners.

The workshop reinforced a critical understanding: communication is not a supporting function. It is a strategic tool for advancing climate action. By sharing stories of innovation, research, entrepreneurship, and community impact, universities can shape public discourse, influence policy, attract partnerships, and inspire broader participation in climate solutions.

Participants left with a clearer roadmap for implementing the Africa Climate Collaborative’s communication strategy and ensuring greater consistency in branding, storytelling, and audience engagement. By speaking with one collective voice, the Collaborative is laying the foundation for greater visibility, collaboration, and influence in the years ahead.

“the Africa Climate Collaborative was established to support higher education institutions in driving climate-positive growth through research, innovation, skills development and youth empowerment. But if we don’t tell the story — if we don’t show what is working, who is leading, what we are learning and what is changing — the impact remains invisible.”

Thuli Montana, Africa Climate Collaborative Lead
 

What We Learned

The Nairobi convening revealed something important about the state of climate action in Africa: the solutions exist. The people driving them are capable, determined, and ready. What is missing are the systems - the financing, the mentorship, the policy support, the institutional connections - that allow those solutions to grow.

Across four days of presentations, panel discussions, and working sessions, several foundational insights crystallised:

1

Climate change in Africa is a development and systems challenge, not a narrow environmental problem. Its impacts span employment, food security, education, fiscal stability, social cohesion, and political resilience. Addressing climate therefore requires structural responses that integrate economic transformation, social inclusion, and institutional reform. As one participant observed: “We cannot treat climate as separate from livelihoods. For Africa, they are the same conversation.”
2

Youth agency consistently strengthens outcomes. Where young people are positioned as co-creators, implementers, network builders, entrepreneurs, and knowledge producers - rather than passive beneficiaries - solutions are more relevant, durable, and scalable. Yet participants were candid about the gap between rhetoric and reality. Youth engagement remains uneven, and NEET youth (Not in Employment, Education or Training) and informal-sector innovators are frequently underrepresented in institutional programmes.
3

Africa does not lack ideas. What it lacks are systems that help those ideas grow. Throughout the convening, participants returned to this central diagnosis. The continent has no shortage of talent, ambition, or innovative solutions. What is needed are coordinated, adaptive systems that can translate these assets into durable, equitable impact at scale. Demand for climate-related skills and entrepreneurship far exceeds institutional capacity.
4

Universities must move beyond siloed programmes toward ecosystem leadership. Isolated centres of excellence, short-term projects, or discipline-bound approaches - even when well-funded - cannot deliver systemic impact. Universities must become convenors: spaces where researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, entrepreneurs, and communities come together to co-create solutions. This requires institutional courage, including the willingness to reform incentives, redistribute power, and invest in coordination capacity. As Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the Association of African Universities, put it: “Universities cannot do this alone. We need industry, government, communities, and young people at the table.”
5

Finance remains the primary constraint, particularly the “missing middle.” Many promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure, and insufficient operational support. Training programmes and pitch competitions are abundant, but access to patient capital, technical expertise, and long-term strategic support remains scarce. The challenge is not the absolute volume of funding but the availability of appropriate financial instruments.
6

Governance coherence, evidence-based learning, and strategic communications are mutually reinforcing systems. A Programme Charter defines purpose and direction. A Theory of Change and MEL framework generate evidence and enable adaptation. Communications amplify impact and build credibility. None functions effectively in isolation. The Collaborative’s first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop underscored another truth: “If you are findable, you are fundable.”
7

Equity and inclusion must be structural, not symbolic. Inclusion was framed not merely as access but as power and value capture - who controls finance, research priorities, enterprise ownership, and decision-making. Those most affected by climate change - youth, women, rural communities, persons with disabilities, displaced populations, and informal-sector workers - must be at the table, not just represented, but empowered to shape agendas and lead. “Inclusion as access is not enough,” one participant observed. “We need inclusion as ownership.”
8

Institutional coordination is as critical as financing. Fragmentation, weak follow-through, and unclear ownership repeatedly undermine promising initiatives. Backbone functions played by the Association of African Universities are essential to success. The Collaborative’s alignment with the African Union’s CESA 2026–2035 framework, and AAU’s designation as lead for the Higher Education, Research, and Innovation Cluster, provides a clear pathway for scaling models beyond pilots into continental systems.
9

Strategic preparation for global policy engagement is essential. COP31 and COP32 and various aligned global platforms represent critical opportunities for African universities and youth leaders to shape continental climate narratives. But meaningful participation requires intentional preparation, realistic expectations, and clear ownership of coordination and messaging. The Collaborative must arrive not as observers but as contributors with evidence-backed positions.

What Comes Next

The conversations in Nairobi were not an endpoint. They were a beginning - the moment when the Collaborative shifted from alignment to activation.

Commitments made in that room are already being followed up.

Venture studios like Delta40 have committed to deepening their engagement with university-based innovators, bridging the gap between research, talent, and market execution. Localized and other industry partners have pledged to strengthen pathways between graduates and green employment opportunities.

The Collaborative is now moving decisively into its next phase of implementation.

Partner institutions will continue advancing research, innovation, climate entrepreneurship, skills development, and youth-focused programmes across the continent.

Upcoming activities will include the rollout of climate-related training programmes, support for green enterprise development, continued research and fellowship activities, expansion of innovation partnerships, and enhanced engagement with policymakers and communities.

Building on the momentum generated in Nairobi, the Collaborative remains committed to translating ambition into action and strengthening pathways for young Africans to contribute meaningfully to climate resilience and sustainable development.
