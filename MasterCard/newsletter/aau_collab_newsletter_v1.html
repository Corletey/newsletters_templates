About the Convening

Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening — Nairobi

The Africa Climate Collaborative (ACC) convened its 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya, from 20–24 April 2026 under the theme “From Ambition to Action: Scaling African Youth-Led Climate Solutions.”

Bringing together representatives from the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the Association of African Universities (AAU), the Mastercard Foundation, youth leaders, researchers, and innovators — the convening served as a strategic platform to accelerate the Collaborative’s vision for climate resilience and sustainable development across Africa.

The convening placed particular emphasis on the role of young people as drivers of climate action and green economic transformation. This newsletter captures key highlights, insights and lessons from the five days in Nairobi.

Youth Leadership

Young Africans Shaping Climate Solutions

At the dedicated youth session, delegates reinforced a central message: young people are not merely beneficiaries of climate programmes — they are active partners and leaders.

Initiatives presented included climate-smart agriculture, digital farmer platforms, maritime cooperatives, and leadership programmes designed to strengthen emerging climate innovators and entrepreneurs. Participants also reflected on barriers: limited access to funding, mentorship, technology and policy support remain significant challenges.

Participants called for structured mechanisms that allow young people to contribute consistently to programme design, implementation and governance.
Climate Leadership

Rethinking Climate Leadership in Africa

The opening session called for a systems-level approach — connecting research, innovation, policy and community action to address climate change as a cross-cutting challenge affecting livelihoods, education, public health, food security and economic development.

While Africa contributes only a small share of global greenhouse gas emissions, it experiences some of the most severe climate-related impacts. Universities were identified as critical ecosystem builders: connecting researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, and communities.

Stronger links between academia, governments, the private sector, and development partners — with greater attention to women, rural communities and persons with disabilities — were identified as critical to an inclusive transition.

“

Africa does not lack talent, ideas, or ambition. The continent’s climate future will depend on its ability to build stronger systems that connect people, institutions, knowledge, and investments.

— Opening Session, ACC 2026 Convening, Nairobi
Foundation Perspective

Ashley Collier, Mastercard Foundation

Ashley Collier, Director for Higher Education for Transformation at the Mastercard Foundation, opened the convening with a high-level overview of the Foundation’s Africa Works strategy. She highlighted the Foundation’s commitment to supporting Africa’s youth and universities in driving climate resilience, innovation and socio-economic transformation.

Collier emphasised the importance of coordinated action to scale climate solutions across the continent, noting that Africa’s youth are central to these efforts. She framed universities as critical hubs for knowledge, research and talent development — capable of fostering climate-smart initiatives, entrepreneurship and policy engagement.

The Foundation’s strategy seeks to integrate climate considerations across education, financial inclusion and workforce development programmes, ensuring young innovators have the resources, mentorship and policy access needed to scale their solutions.

She encouraged participants to collaborate, innovate and share lessons learned to accelerate systemic change and build long-term capacity for climate action across Africa.
 
Green Jobs Project

Preparing Youth for Africa’s Green Economy

University of Cambridge  ·  Two-Year Research Initiative

A youth-centred research initiative where young people were not merely participants but active partners in shaping research questions, collecting data, analysing findings and sharing results. The project identified vast opportunities in renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, waste management, and ecosystem restoration.

Key barriers surfaced: low awareness of green career pathways, limited access to finance for young entrepreneurs, gender inequalities, and social perceptions limiting uptake in sectors like agriculture and waste management.

Universities must move beyond traditional academic models and equip students with entrepreneurial, interdisciplinary and practical skills, integrating sustainability across teaching and research and recognising indigenous knowledge systems as valuable contributors.
Climate Innovation

Turning Ideas into Action: Universities and Climate Innovation

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the AAU, stressed that while institutions are increasingly investing in climate-related research, innovation hubs and entrepreneurship programmes, stronger enabling environments are needed to develop, test and scale innovations for real-world impact.

Young entrepreneur Laura Kasuni Wathe noted that many graduates struggle to transition from education to enterprise, calling for greater emphasis on mentorship, systems thinking, leadership development and community engagement.

The concept of “living laboratories” — where universities work directly with communities to develop solutions in real-world settings — was highlighted as a powerful model. Inclusion of women, persons with disabilities, and underserved communities was emphasised as essential.
 
Delta40 Site Visit

From Ideas to Impact: Lessons on Scaling Climate Innovation

The Delta40 site visit gave participants a firsthand look at the realities of climate entrepreneurship. Discussions highlighted a common challenge: innovation is not the problem — execution is. A key focus was the “valley of death” — the stage where many ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure and insufficient operational support.

Many founders described themselves as “over-mentored and under-capitalised”: while training programmes and pitch competitions are abundant, access to patient capital, technical expertise and strategic business support remains limited.

The Delta40 model offers a different approach — working closely with entrepreneurs over extended periods, providing embedded support in finance, operations and commercialisation. Discussions also highlighted the gap between research and commercialisation within African universities.

Africa has no shortage of talent, ambition or ideas. What is needed are stronger systems that support execution, provide long-term backing for entrepreneurs, and connect research with real-world application.
 
Communications & Branding

Strengthening Universities’ Collective Voice for Climate Resilience

Communications leads from all partner institutions gathered in Nairobi to align on strategic communications, branding and impact storytelling. The central challenge: valuable research and innovation remain underrepresented due to fragmented communication efforts.

The ACC’s first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop, opened by ACC Lead Thuli Montana, operationalised the Collaborative’s communication strategy — covering storytelling frameworks, audience engagement, branding guidelines, and monitoring of impact. The workshop concluded with a renewed commitment to strengthen coordination and amplify impact with a unified voice.

Through collaboration, strategic storytelling and inclusive engagement, partner institutions are working to amplify a collective voice for climate resilience across the continent.

Key Takeaways from the 2026 Convening

1

Systems-level solutions: Addressing climate challenges demands stronger connections between research, policy, innovation, financing and community action.
2

Youth at the centre: Expanding access to skills development, entrepreneurship support, mentorship and financing is essential for unlocking the potential of young Africans as climate leaders.
3

Universities’ critical role: Higher education institutions are uniquely positioned to generate knowledge, nurture innovation and prepare young people for the green economy.
4

Innovation ecosystems: Effective solutions require long-term support systems that enable ideas to move from research and pilot stages to sustainable real-world impact.
5

Strategic communication is vital: Amplifying African-led climate stories and showcasing impact can strengthen partnerships, influence policy and inspire action. The ACC’s first Communications and Branding Workshop reinforced the importance of a coordinated collective voice. “If you are findable, you are fundable.”
6

Finance: the “missing middle”: Many promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure and insufficient operational support. Training and pitch competitions are abundant — but access to patient capital, technical expertise and long-term strategic backing remains scarce.
7

Equity and inclusion must be structural, not symbolic: Inclusion is not merely about access — it demands power and value capture. Those most affected by climate change, including youth, women, persons with disabilities and informal-sector workers, must be empowered to shape agendas and lead.
8

Institutional coordination is as critical as financing: Fragmentation and weak follow-through repeatedly undermine promising initiatives. The AAU’s backbone role, and the Collaborative’s alignment with the African Union’s CESA 2026–2035 framework, provides a clear pathway to scale beyond pilots into continental systems.
9

Strategic preparation for global policy engagement: COP31, COP32 and aligned global platforms represent critical opportunities for African universities and youth leaders to shape continental climate narratives. Meaningful participation requires intentional preparation, clear messaging ownership, and arriving not as observers but as contributors with evidence-backed positions.

Moving from Ambition to Action — What Comes Next

As the Africa Climate Collaborative enters the next phase of implementation, partner institutions will continue advancing research, innovation, climate entrepreneurship, skills development and youth-focused programmes across the continent. Upcoming activities will include the rollout of climate-related training programmes, support for green enterprise development, continued research and fellowship activities, expansion of innovation partnerships, and enhanced engagement with policymakers and communities.

Building on the momentum generated in Nairobi, the Collaborative remains committed to translating ambition into action and strengthening pathways for young Africans to contribute meaningfully to climate resilience and sustainable development.
