About the Climate Resilience & Sustainability Collaborative

The Climate Resilience & Sustainability Collaborative brings together the University of Cambridge, University of Ghana, Makerere University and the University of Cape Town to strengthen research, data and capabilities for green economies across Africa. The initiative is anchored by the Association of African Universities as backbone organisation and CoCreative as technical partner, with the Mastercard Foundation as lead funder.

Together, the network is working toward the Mastercard Foundation’s goal of enabling 30 million young people — particularly young women — to secure dignified and fulfilling work by 2030. The Collaborative drives evidence-based decision-making, innovation, and the creation of dignified green jobs for young people across the continent.

Accra Workshop: Mapping Shared Purpose, 22–25 April

Partners of the Collaborative gathered in Accra from 22–25 April 2026 to map the Collaborative’s shared purpose, define critical shifts and systems-change thinking, and stand up a Stewardship Team with a representative from each university. It marked the formal launch of structured collaboration across five institutions spanning three continents.

The AAU was contracted to lead logistics and post-conference documentation, and to develop a draft Theory of Change and a Monitoring, Evaluation, Learning & Adaptation (MELA) framework to guide the Collaborative’s implementation through to 2032.

Mastercard Foundation Commits $14M to Continental Climate Fellowship

The Mastercard Foundation has committed USD 14 million over five years to fund 200 doctoral and post-doctoral African climate scholars across the four partner universities. The programme represents the largest single private investment ever channelled through the AAU, and is designed to build a sustained pipeline of African-led climate research and policy expertise.

At least 60% of fellowships are reserved for women researchers, and a further 20% for scholars from fragile and conflict-affected states. Fellows will be embedded in cross-institutional research clusters — a deliberate move away from siloed grant-making toward sustained collaborative authorship of African climate science.

The Four Work Streams Driving Change

01 — Workforce Development

Degrees, diplomas and leadership skills that move young people into green jobs and climate innovation.

02 — Entrepreneurship

Generating, accelerating and scaling climate-smart ventures, with a clear path to commercialisation.

03 — Research & Innovation

Regional centres of excellence delivering interdisciplinary research and climate-focused teaching.

04 — Networks, Policy & Learning

A pan-African “network of networks” transforming the climate policy agenda across member states.

Looking Ahead: The 2026 Africa Climate Collaborative Convening

As work plans move into implementation (2026–2032), partners are preparing for the next Africa Climate Collaborative Convening — an annual moment to share research findings, monitor progress and grow the continent’s climate “network of networks.” The inaugural convening will be hosted in Accra and will bring together all five partner institutions alongside African researchers, policymakers and youth delegates.

Over five days, delegates will operationalise four shared deliverables: a continental climate data atlas, a 200-fellow doctoral pipeline, a joint policy brief to the African Union, and an open-access curriculum on climate justice to be adopted by every AAU member university by 2028.
