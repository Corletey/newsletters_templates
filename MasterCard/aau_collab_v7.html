About the Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening

The Africa Climate Collaborative (ACC) convened its 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya, from 20–24 April 2026 under the theme “From Ambition to Action: Scaling African Youth-Led Climate Solutions.” Bringing together representatives from the University of Cambridge, the University of Cape Town, the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the Association of African Universities (AAU), the Mastercard Foundation, youth leaders, researchers, innovators, and ecosystem partners, the convening served as a strategic platform to accelerate the implementation of the Collaborative’s vision for climate resilience and sustainable development across Africa.

The convening placed particular emphasis on the role of young people as drivers of climate action and green economic transformation. Discussions focused on climate innovation and entrepreneurship, research and knowledge generation, workforce development, policy engagement, and strengthening partnerships capable of scaling African-led climate solutions.

This special newsletter edition captures key highlights, insights, and lessons from the convening — showcasing how universities, development partners, innovators, and young leaders are working together to translate ambition into action and build a more resilient and sustainable future for Africa.

Youth Leadership at the Forefront: Young Africans Shaping Climate Solutions

Young people are increasingly at the forefront of climate action across Africa, developing innovative solutions, driving community initiatives, and advocating for a more sustainable future. At the Africa Climate Collaborative Convening 2026 in Nairobi, Kenya, youth leaders, innovators, and programme representatives came together to reflect on the challenges facing their communities and explore practical pathways for scaling youth-led climate action.

The dedicated youth session reinforced a central message that echoed throughout the convening: young people are not merely beneficiaries of climate programmes, they are active partners and leaders in shaping Africa’s climate future. Participants highlighted the value of having a platform where their experiences, perspectives, and ideas could directly inform discussions and contribute to the Collaborative’s broader agenda.

Across the session, young delegates shared examples of initiatives already making a difference in their communities. These included climate-smart agriculture and agroforestry programmes, digital platforms connecting farmers to markets, maritime cooperatives promoting sustainable livelihoods, and leadership programmes designed to strengthen the capacity of emerging climate innovators and entrepreneurs. Together, these initiatives demonstrated the creativity and determination with which young Africans are responding to climate, environmental, and economic challenges.

While celebrating these successes, participants also reflected on the barriers that continue to limit youth participation and impact. Limited access to funding, mentorship, technology, and policy support remain significant challenges for many young innovators. Delegates also noted the need for clearer pathways for engagement and greater opportunities for young people to contribute meaningfully to decision-making processes at local, national, and continental levels.

A recurring theme throughout the discussion was the importance of moving from ambition to action. Participants stressed that many promising youth-led solutions already exist, but greater investment is needed to help these initiatives grow and achieve wider impact. Strengthening mentorship networks, expanding access to financing, and creating more opportunities for collaboration were identified as essential steps in supporting the next generation of climate leaders.

Partnerships emerged as a critical enabler of success. Participants highlighted the important role that governments, universities, development partners, media organisations, and private sector actors can play in supporting youth-led innovation. Institutions such as the Association of African Universities (AAU) and partner universities were recognised for their potential to strengthen research-to-practice linkages, provide capacity-building opportunities, and create environments where young people can develop and scale their ideas.

The session also underscored the importance of ensuring that youth engagement extends beyond individual events and consultations. Participants called for more structured mechanisms that allow young people to contribute consistently to programme design, implementation, and governance. Such approaches, they noted, would help ensure that youth perspectives remain embedded within climate initiatives and development strategies.

As discussions concluded, participants agreed that empowering young people is not simply an investment in the future, it is an investment in the present. Across the continent, young Africans are already leading efforts to address climate challenges, create sustainable livelihoods, and build resilient communities.

The Nairobi convening reaffirmed that when provided with the right support, resources, and opportunities, young people have the capacity to drive transformative change. By strengthening youth leadership, fostering collaboration, and investing in innovation, the Africa Climate Collaborative is helping to create pathways for a new generation of climate leaders to shape Africa’s sustainable future.

“Africa does not lack talent, ideas, or ambition. The continent’s climate future will depend on its ability to build stronger systems that connect people, institutions, knowledge, and investments.”

From Ambition to Action: Rethinking Climate Leadership in Africa

The Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening opened in Nairobi, Kenya, with a compelling call for a new approach to climate action — one that moves beyond isolated interventions towards coordinated, systems-level solutions.

Bringing together academic leaders, development practitioners, innovators, and development partners, the opening session explored how Africa can strengthen climate resilience by connecting research, innovation, policy, and community action. Participants agreed that climate change is not simply an environmental issue but a complex challenge affecting livelihoods, education, public health, food security, and economic development.

Moderated by Prof. Frederick Ato Armah, Director of Research and Programmes at the Association of African Universities (AAU), the discussion emphasised the need for collaboration across institutions and sectors to achieve lasting impact.

A central theme of the session was the importance of viewing climate change as a systems challenge. Participants noted that while Africa contributes only a small share of global greenhouse gas emissions, it continues to experience some of the most severe climate-related impacts, including food insecurity, displacement, and economic vulnerability. Addressing these challenges, they argued, requires integrated solutions that cut across disciplines, sectors, and institutions.

The discussion also highlighted the continent’s greatest asset — its youthful population. Young people are already driving climate innovation, entrepreneurship, and community-based solutions across Africa. However, participants stressed that greater efforts are needed to expand access to financing, mentorship, leadership opportunities, and decision-making spaces to enable youth to contribute meaningfully to climate action.

Universities were identified as critical actors in this transformation. Beyond their traditional roles in teaching and research, higher education institutions were encouraged to serve as ecosystem builders by connecting researchers, policymakers, industry leaders, entrepreneurs, and communities. Such partnerships can help accelerate the translation of research into practical solutions while strengthening climate innovation across the continent.

Participants also reflected on the persistent challenges facing climate innovators. Many promising ideas struggle to move beyond pilot stages due to limited access to financing, particularly the patient and flexible capital required to support early-stage ventures. Bridging this gap, they noted, will be essential to scaling locally driven climate solutions.

Another key issue raised was the need to strengthen links between research, policy, and practice. While universities and innovators continue to generate valuable knowledge, translating research findings into policies, products, and community solutions remains a challenge. Stronger collaboration between academia, governments, the private sector, and development partners was identified as critical to closing this gap.

The session further underscored the importance of ensuring that climate action is inclusive. Participants called for greater attention to the needs and contributions of women, rural communities, persons with disabilities, and other groups that are often underrepresented in climate decision-making processes.

By empowering young people, strengthening universities, and fostering collaboration across sectors, Africa can move beyond fragmented interventions and accelerate the transition towards a more resilient and sustainable future.

Preparing Youth for Africa’s Green Economy: The Role of Universities

As Africa seeks to build climate-resilient economies and create sustainable livelihoods for its growing youth population, a key question is emerging: Are young people equipped with the skills needed to thrive in the green economy?

This question was at the heart of a session showcasing findings from the Green Jobs Project, a two-year research initiative led by the University of Cambridge in collaboration with partner universities and youth organisations across Africa. Presented during the Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening in Nairobi, the session explored opportunities and barriers within Africa’s green economy and examined the role of higher education in preparing young people for the jobs of the future.

A distinguishing feature of the project was its youth-centred approach. Young people were not simply research participants but active partners in shaping research questions, collecting data, analysing findings, and sharing results. This ensured that the experiences and perspectives of young people remained central throughout the project.

The research highlighted the vast potential of the green economy, identifying opportunities across sectors such as renewable energy, sustainable agriculture, waste management, ecosystem restoration, and environmental services. However, it also revealed significant barriers that continue to limit youth participation.

One of the most pressing challenges is low awareness of green jobs and the opportunities they offer. Many young people remain unfamiliar with career pathways in emerging green industries. Access to finance was also identified as a major obstacle, particularly for young entrepreneurs seeking to establish and grow climate-focused businesses.

The findings further revealed that social perceptions continue to influence career choices. Sectors such as agriculture and waste management are often viewed as less desirable despite their growing importance within the green economy. Gender inequalities, limited access to resources, and persistent skills gaps also continue to restrict opportunities for many young people.

To address these challenges, the project called for greater investment in skills development, entrepreneurship support, and climate-focused financing mechanisms. Participants emphasised the need for education and training systems that are better aligned with labour market demands, as well as policies that encourage innovation and green enterprise development.

The findings sparked a broader discussion on the role of higher education institutions in Africa’s green transition. During a panel discussion that followed, participants reflected on whether universities are preparing students for today’s green jobs or for the future green economy that Africa is striving to build.

A key message emerging from the discussion was that universities must move beyond traditional academic models and equip students with practical, entrepreneurial, and interdisciplinary skills. Stronger partnerships between universities, industry, governments, and communities were also identified as essential for ensuring that graduates are prepared to respond to evolving workforce needs.

Panelists further highlighted the importance of integrating sustainability across teaching, research, and innovation while recognising indigenous knowledge systems as valuable contributors to environmental stewardship and climate solutions.

Ultimately, the session reinforced the critical role of higher education institutions in shaping Africa’s green future. As demand for green skills continues to grow, universities have an important responsibility to prepare young people not only for existing jobs, but also for the opportunities and industries that will drive the continent’s sustainable development in the years ahead.

Turning Ideas into Action: Universities and Climate Innovation in Africa

How can African universities move beyond generating knowledge to creating solutions that address real-world climate challenges?

This question shaped a high-level panel discussion during the Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya. Bringing together representatives from academia, industry, development organisations, and youth entrepreneurship, the session explored how higher education institutions can strengthen climate innovation, support entrepreneurship, and prepare young people to lead Africa’s green transition.

Held under the theme “Climate Innovation in Action: Research, Education and Entrepreneurial Opportunities Across Africa,” the discussion highlighted the critical role universities can play in translating research into practical solutions that address Africa’s climate challenges.

A key message emerging from the panel was the need for universities to strengthen connections between research, industry, communities, and policymakers. While African universities continue to produce valuable research and innovative ideas, many of these innovations struggle to move beyond academic settings and into practical application.

Prof. Olusola Oyewole, Secretary-General of the Association of African Universities (AAU), noted that many institutions are increasingly investing in climate-related research, innovation hubs, entrepreneurship programmes, and skills development initiatives. However, he emphasised the importance of strengthening partnerships and creating enabling environments that allow innovations to be developed, tested, and scaled for impact.

The discussion also highlighted the importance of equipping students with practical skills that extend beyond traditional academic training. Sharing her experience as a young entrepreneur, Laura Kasuni Wathe observed that many graduates face challenges when transitioning from education to enterprise development. She called for greater emphasis on mentorship, systems thinking, leadership development, and community engagement to help young people transform ideas into sustainable ventures.

Another recurring theme was the importance of stronger university-industry collaboration. Panelists stressed that partnerships with businesses, governments, and innovation ecosystems can help bridge the gap between research and implementation while creating opportunities for internships, incubation, commercialisation, and enterprise growth.

Participants also explored the concept of “living laboratories,” where universities work directly with communities, entrepreneurs, and other stakeholders to develop and test solutions in real-world settings. Such approaches, they noted, can ensure that innovations are grounded in local realities and respond to the needs of the communities they are intended to serve.

Beyond technology and entrepreneurship, the discussion challenged participants to rethink what innovation means. Speakers highlighted the value of indigenous knowledge and locally developed practices in addressing environmental challenges, emphasising the need for universities to engage more intentionally with communities and traditional knowledge holders.

Inclusion was another central theme. Panelists underscored the importance of ensuring that women, persons with disabilities, displaced populations, and young people from underserved communities are meaningfully represented within climate innovation ecosystems. Building resilient and sustainable solutions, they argued, requires the active participation of those most affected by climate-related challenges.

The role of supportive ecosystems was also emphasised. While access to finance remains important, participants noted that young innovators often require a broader range of support, including mentorship, technical guidance, market access, policy support, and networking opportunities.

As the session concluded, participants agreed that universities have a unique opportunity to shape Africa’s climate future. By fostering innovation, strengthening partnerships, embracing diverse knowledge systems, and empowering young people to lead, higher education institutions can play a pivotal role in advancing climate resilience and sustainable development across the continent.

Ultimately, the discussion reinforced a key message of the convening: addressing climate challenges requires more than research alone. It demands collaboration, inclusion, and a commitment to transforming ideas into practical solutions that create lasting impact for communities across Africa.

“Africa does not lack ideas. What we lack are systems that help those ideas grow.”

From Ideas to Impact: Lessons from Delta40 on Scaling Climate Innovation in Africa

This message resonated throughout the Delta40 site visit during the Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening in Nairobi, Kenya. The visit provided participants with a firsthand look at the realities of climate entrepreneurship and the challenges innovators face in transforming promising ideas into scalable solutions.

Held under the theme “From Ambition to Action: Scaling African Youth-Led Climate Solutions,” the engagement moved beyond theory to explore what it takes to build successful climate ventures in Africa. Discussions with founders, innovators, and ecosystem builders highlighted a common challenge: innovation is not the problem — execution is.

A key focus of the visit was the so-called “valley of death,” the stage where many promising ventures struggle to grow due to limited financing, weak infrastructure, and insufficient operational support. This challenge is particularly acute in sectors such as clean energy, climate technology, and sustainable food systems, where entrepreneurs often require significant capital and long-term support to scale their solutions.

Many founders described themselves as “over-mentored and under-capitalised,” reflecting a common frustration within the entrepreneurial ecosystem. While training programmes and pitch competitions are abundant, access to patient capital, technical expertise, and strategic business support remains limited. As a result, many innovative ventures struggle to move beyond the early stages of development.

The Delta40 model offers a different approach. Rather than providing short-term acceleration, the venture studio works closely with entrepreneurs over extended periods, offering embedded support in areas such as finance, operations, and commercialisation. This hands-on model helps ventures navigate complex challenges while building the capacity needed for long-term growth.

The discussions also highlighted the gap between research and commercialisation within African universities. Participants noted that many researchers develop innovative solutions with significant potential but often lack the structures, partnerships, and support needed to bring those ideas to market. Strengthening collaboration between academia and industry was identified as a critical step in ensuring that research can translate into practical impact.

Youth leadership featured prominently throughout the engagement. Across the ventures showcased, young innovators are already developing solutions in areas ranging from climate adaptation and clean energy to food systems transformation. However, participants emphasised that meaningful youth participation must be accompanied by sustained investment, mentorship, and access to opportunities that enable young entrepreneurs to thrive.

Partnerships also emerged as a critical driver of success. Existing collaborations between Delta40 and institutions such as the University of Cambridge demonstrate the value of linking research, innovation, and enterprise development. Participants noted that expanding similar partnerships across African universities could help create stronger pathways for climate innovation and venture growth.

The visit ultimately reinforced a central message of the convening: Africa has no shortage of talent, ambition, or innovative ideas. What is needed are stronger systems that support execution, provide long-term backing for entrepreneurs, and connect research with real-world application.

As climate challenges continue to grow, building these enabling ecosystems will be essential to ensuring that promising ideas do not remain concepts, but evolve into solutions capable of delivering lasting impact across the continent.

Strengthening Universities’ Collective Voice for Climate Resilience

Effective climate action depends not only on innovative solutions but also on the ability to communicate those solutions to the people who need them most. Recognising this, communications leads and programme representatives from partner institutions of the Africa Climate Collaborative convened in Nairobi, Kenya, to strengthen alignment on strategic communications, branding, and impact storytelling.

The session, held as part of the Africa Climate Collaborative 2026 Convening, brought together representatives from the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the University of Cape Town, the University of Cambridge, the Association of African Universities (AAU), and the Mastercard Foundation. Discussions focused on how the Collaborative can amplify African-led climate solutions, increase visibility for research and innovation, and ensure that climate knowledge reaches policymakers, entrepreneurs, communities, and young people across the continent.

A central theme of the engagement was the need for a more coordinated approach to communication. Participants acknowledged that valuable research, innovation, and community impact often remain underrepresented due to fragmented communication efforts. By strengthening collaboration and adopting a shared storytelling framework, the institutions aim to increase the reach and influence of their collective work.

The University of Ghana highlighted plans to strengthen project visibility through a cohesive brand identity, share impact stories from researchers and fellows, and promote youth-focused climate initiatives and green enterprise success stories. Makerere University emphasised inclusion and accessibility, with a focus on ensuring that climate opportunities reach underserved and vulnerable young people while strengthening systems for measuring and communicating impact.

The University of Cambridge outlined efforts to enhance digital visibility through the development of a central platform for showcasing grants, opportunities, and impact stories. The institution also highlighted initiatives that connect climate innovation, youth employment, and entrepreneurship, demonstrating how partnerships can create practical opportunities for young Africans.

Meanwhile, the University of Cape Town underscored the importance of promoting climate education, innovation, and interdisciplinary research. Through initiatives such as its Graduate School of Climate and Climate Action Innovation Lab, the university is helping to nurture the next generation of climate researchers and innovators while generating impact stories that can inspire wider engagement.

Despite their different priorities, the institutions identified several common challenges. These include translating complex research into accessible narratives, producing high-quality content consistently, measuring communication impact, and ensuring effective coordination across partners.

Participants also highlighted the need to strengthen science communication and impact storytelling skills, improve knowledge-sharing mechanisms, and establish clearer processes for collaborative content development. Such efforts, they noted, will be essential in ensuring that climate research and innovation are communicated in ways that resonate with diverse audiences.

The session reinforced the understanding that communication is not merely a supporting function but a strategic tool for advancing climate action. By sharing stories of innovation, research, entrepreneurship, and community impact, universities can help shape public discourse, influence policy, attract partnerships, and inspire broader participation in climate solutions.

As the Africa Climate Collaborative continues to expand its reach and impact, a stronger and more unified communications approach will play a vital role in ensuring that African-led climate solutions gain the visibility they deserve. Through collaboration, strategic storytelling, and inclusive engagement, the partner institutions are working to amplify a collective voice for climate resilience across the continent.

Africa Climate Collaborative Strengthens Communication and Branding Strategy Through Landmark Workshop

The Africa Climate Collaborative took an important step towards strengthening its collective identity and visibility with the convening of its first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop in Nairobi, Kenya.

Bringing together communications professionals, programme coordinators, and institutional representatives from partner universities, the workshop provided a platform to align communication priorities, strengthen storytelling approaches, and enhance collaboration across the network. Participants included representatives from the University of Ghana, Makerere University, the University of Cape Town, the University of Cambridge, the Association of African Universities (AAU), and the Mastercard Foundation.

Opening the session, ACC Lead Thuli Montana underscored the importance of communication in advancing the Collaborative’s mission. She noted that the ACC was established to support higher education institutions in driving climate-positive growth through research, innovation, skills development, and youth empowerment. As the initiative enters a new phase of implementation, she emphasised the need to intentionally capture and share stories that demonstrate impact and inspire action.

Throughout the workshop, participants explored how strategic communication can help amplify African-led climate solutions and increase awareness of the work being undertaken across partner institutions. Discussions focused on ensuring that research findings, innovation initiatives, and youth-focused programmes are communicated effectively to policymakers, development partners, communities, and young people.

A key theme emerging from the engagement was the importance of speaking with one collective voice. Participants recognised that while each institution has its own priorities and audiences, a coordinated communication approach can strengthen the visibility and influence of the Collaborative as a whole. Institutions were encouraged to actively share and amplify one another’s stories, creating a stronger platform for promoting climate action and innovation across the continent.

The workshop also provided an opportunity to operationalise the Collaborative’s communication strategy, which has been developed through extensive consultation among partners over the past year. Sessions focused on storytelling frameworks, audience engagement, communication planning, monitoring and evaluation, and the practical application of branding guidelines across a range of communication materials.

Particular attention was given to establishing a consistent visual identity for the Collaborative. Participants reviewed branding protocols covering digital content, publications, events, promotional materials, and institutional communications. While recognising the diversity of participating institutions, facilitators stressed that consistency in branding and messaging is essential to building a recognisable and trusted identity for the ACC.

The discussions further highlighted the growing importance of impact storytelling. Participants agreed that communication should move beyond reporting activities and instead focus on demonstrating how climate research, innovation, and skills development initiatives are creating tangible benefits for communities and young people across Africa. By sharing compelling stories of change, the Collaborative aims to strengthen engagement, attract partnerships, and showcase the role of African universities in advancing climate resilience and sustainable development.

The workshop concluded with a renewed commitment among partner institutions to strengthen coordination, share knowledge, and amplify the impact of climate-focused initiatives across the Collaborative. Participants left with a clearer roadmap for implementing the ACC’s communication strategy and ensuring greater consistency in branding, storytelling, and audience engagement.

As the Collaborative advances its work in research, innovation, skills development, and youth empowerment, strategic communication will play a critical role in connecting stakeholders, showcasing impact, and elevating African-led climate solutions. By investing in stronger communication systems and collective storytelling, partner institutions are laying the foundation for greater visibility, collaboration, and influence in the years ahead.

Key Takeaways from the 2026 Africa Climate Collaborative Convening

The 2026 Africa Climate Collaborative Convening reinforced a shared commitment to advancing African-led solutions to climate challenges through collaboration, innovation, and youth empowerment. Across discussions, workshops, and site engagements, several key messages emerged:

01 — Systems-Level Solutions

Addressing climate challenges demands stronger connections between research, policy, innovation, financing, and community action.

02 — Youth at the Centre

Expanding access to skills development, entrepreneurship support, mentorship, and financing is essential for unlocking youth potential.

03 — Universities’ Critical Role

Higher education institutions are uniquely positioned to generate knowledge, nurture innovation, and prepare young people for the green economy.

04 — Innovation Ecosystems

Effective climate solutions require long-term support systems that enable ideas to move from research and pilot stages to sustainable impact.

05 — Strategic Communication is Vital

Amplifying African-led climate stories and showcasing impact can strengthen partnerships, influence policy, and inspire action across the continent. The ACC’s first in-person Communications and Branding Workshop in Nairobi reinforced the importance of a coordinated collective voice.

The convening also highlighted the importance of collaboration among universities, development partners, policymakers, industry, and communities in building a climate-resilient and prosperous future for Africa.

Moving from Ambition to Action

As the Africa Climate Collaborative enters the next phase of implementation, partner institutions will continue advancing research, innovation, climate entrepreneurship, skills development, and youth-focused programmes across the continent.

Upcoming activities will include the rollout of climate-related training programmes, support for green enterprise development, continued research and fellowship activities, expansion of innovation partnerships, and enhanced engagement with policymakers and communities.

Building on the momentum generated in Nairobi, the Collaborative remains committed to translating ambition into action and strengthening pathways for young Africans to contribute meaningfully to climate resilience and sustainable development.
